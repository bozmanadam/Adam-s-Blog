Keep your promises.

"Be the people and graduates that always welcome strangers at your front door."

-Sherman Alexie on May 14, 2017

What's the next step?

The advice of our commencement speaker, Sherman Alexie, “remember your promises.” A sobering tone that rang through the floor of the Spokane Arena, Alexie recalled his childhood as a poor Indian boy. He recognized his success as an author could only be attributed to those catalysts of his growth, those people that made him promises.

“They said, ‘You work hard, stranger, and you will graduate. You work hard, stranger, and we’ll help you get to the next rung on the ladder... That town made me promises and it kept them.”

With a mixture of expressions across the faces of students nearest me on the floor, I saw a combination disinterest, shame, and provocation. This adequately summed up the varying but general dispositions of Gonzaga students regarding racial condemnations and controversial subjects. Mr. Alexie’s message was not one of promise and prestige, but it was one of responsibility and integrity. Fittingly, it was that this addressed our recent graduates.

It’s been a little over two weeks since I left the stadium that day, and my prospects for work are still minimal. Oddly, or perhaps intentionally, the message given that day rings clearer than ever right now. In its continual circulation through my thoughts the idea of promises calms my nerves of the future. The idea of promises provides a goal for today, and for tomorrow. It is a responsibility, and it is one I hope to never mistreat. My responsibility remains to those people that help me and those people that may need my help. Without knowing anything else of my next few months, I know that I will continue to be a person that welcomes strangers at my front door.
